The Work of J.R.R. Tolkien

Putting aside the (arguably true) notion that J.R.R. Tolkien is among the most influential fantasy writers of all time, it is worth mentioning the intent of his writing and what it was meant to represent. While it may seem presumptuous to impose a definition on the intentions of another person, Tolkien's readers have a wealth of published personal communications between Tolkien, his son, and his publisher that clearly outline an overarching purpose for the lore of Middle-Earth.

In those letters and correspondences, Tolkien proposed the idea that he would endeavour to write a creation myth or extended mythos for the British Isles, which he felt possessed no real myths of their own. Being a philologist and student of history, Tolkien was strongly influenced by the myth-building of Scandinavian cultures and traditional Biblical structures, both of which he borrowed heavily from.
